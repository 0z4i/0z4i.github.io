Study Case: Verify identity and medical credentials for healthcare platforms

Brief

With the digitalization of all sectors, it is very common to search for health services on digital platforms like Doctoralia, but is all the information declared by the users (health professionals) that offer their services completely real? The following OSINT investigation deals with an identity, foothold, license, and credentials validation to evaluate the truthfulness of the posted information in this case, a Mexican psychologist.

Key judgments

1: The target identity is valid, and the professional licence he declared exists and is related to some of the offered services, and it can be related and confirmed with some of his digital footprint.

2: Some of the elements mentioned in the educational history have no proof, and the declared degrees don't have an official record.

3: The target in question is using an academic qualification that does not correspond to the one they publicly advertise when promoting their services.

4: The target promotes and carries out activities involving substances declared illegal under Mexican law.

5: The subject has no record (in the sources consulted) of a criminal history or previous legal problems, and furthermore, his public reputation, based on the feedback he has received, is generally good.

Exploiting the Profile

Doctoralia is a digital platform where health professionals advertise their services. In this case a psychologist declared in his profile with a master's degree; from the profile it was possible to extract the following:

  • Full name
  • 5 addresses of clinics and attention sites
  • Professional Credential ID
  • Contact Phone Number
  • Own organisation name
  • List of promoting services
  • Potential residency state

Using the content posted on Doctoralia by the target, I identified an ad where the phone number was extracted next to an organisation name. Also, in other posts with pictures of the target performing consulting and mentioning other clinics, this may make sense with the different clinics mentioned on the profile; one of them is a medical clinic with X-ray and clinical laboratory services.

First Validation

Taking the Professional Credential ID to validate it on the official site https://cedulaprofesional.sep.gob.mx to check if it's real and it returns a record with:
- Credential ID
- Full name
- Gender
- Institution (School)
- Grade and career
- School state
- Registration year

The full name corresponds to the name in the Doctoralia profile, but I found a gap of 5 years between the career end year mentioned in Doctoralia and the professional credential ID year registration; it could be for many reasons.

Also this record id for a bachelor's degree, not a master's degree as mentioned on Doctoralia.

Pivoting to Social Media

Using the previous insights, I searched for the target on Facebook; due to his approximate age, it's possible to find an account, and I found one account where the account name has the first name and first last name of the Doctoralia profile, and the picture has a very similar man.

Checking for the posts, one was identified as being about a job offer posted by the target seeking laboratory staff; it makes sense with the previously found laboratory, and this post has the same phone number used in Doctoralia posts as a contact method. Doing a cross-reference is useful to confirm the Facebook profile ownership, and it also expands the results with a new email.

With a higher degree of certainty on the profile ownership, I take the linked social profiles in the biography, like TikTok and Instagram, to expand the foothold network of the target. I spotted the repetition of the first identified phone number of the target in multiple posts where they promote their service, like psychology consulting or psychotherapy.

The official identity

Now that the phone number is observed in a recent post, it may imply that it is the current or one of the current targets' phone numbers, and then I search for a phone record about it using published information.

"Published data" refers to any data exposed in the public domain, including data breaches from originally private databases. Unlike public data (official/governmental), it encompasses compromised assets accessible for intelligence.

This phone number is linked to the record with the same name as the Doctoralia profile; this may partially confirm the target's legal identity. Also, it expands the information about the target with:
- RFC (Mexican Fiscal ID)
- Address
- City
- State
- Contact Phone Number (Used in the phone number line registration)

The city and state correspond with the location declared on the Doctoralia profile, and the RFC can be used to search for other identity records also using published records. In this case, one citizen record was uncovered, now spotting the following:
- CURP (citizen ID)
- Birthdate
- Gender
- Address
- State
- Birth state

Crossing the data at this point, I identified that:
- Birth state is the same as that found in the professional credential ID record.
- Same name
- Residency state is the same as mentioned in the Doctoralia profile.
- Phone number repeated in post on social profiles

For extra validation, I use the CURP to search for other credential IDs because the found ones have a different grade than Doctoralia declared, but the CURP in the citizen record doesn't have any professional record.

In Mexico it is possible that one citizen has more than one "unique" ID; it can be extracted on the official RENAPO site, https://www.gob.mx/curp. Here using a CURP allows us to get more than one CURP (not for all citizens). In this case the target has 3 CURPs linked, 2 linked by corrections, and one of them has a different birth date (different month).

With the new extracted CURPs, I uncover that the ID with a different birthdate is linked to the professional ID.

At this point I can infer with a high degree of certainty that the target's declared identity is real.

Educational History

The professional credential ID now validated is linked to a bachelor's degree in psychology, but the target declares that it has a master's degree. Also, there is another important thing: the professional ID was registered 5 years after the end of the bachelor's degree.

In the educational history declared on Doctoralia, the target mentions a master's degree in Gestalt psychotherapy. I reviewed the institution for this grade, and it's real; it's registered in the Mexican regulatory agency RVOE https://sirvoes.sep.gob.mx/sirvoes/mvc/consultas , but the target doesn't have a record linked to studies at this institute.

The target adds to his educational history only 4 elements with dates and 6 other non-chronological events, 3 without mention of the institute, and only one of them (a bachelor's degree in psychology) has a record of a professional license.

Laws and regulations

The following is based only on the current Mexican laws: First of all, a public statement regarding a level of education in which the objective is not present, only having a bachelor's degree and mentioning a master's, is in accordance with Article 250, Section 2, of the Federal Criminal Code and is an activity considered to be the unauthorized practice of a profession, which could constitute a violation of that law.

I'm not a lawyer, so I may make mistakes in interpreting the law.

Another question is, do the targets have some posts on Doctoralia, Facebook, and TikTok about psilocybin-assisted therapies and ceremonies, also referring to them as "sacred mushrooms," "little mushrooms," etc.

This substance is prohibited under Mexican health law, as set forth in Articles 245, 246, and 248, It prohibits the use of said substance for all of the following activities:
- planting
- cultivation
- harvesting
- processing
- preparation
- packaging
- acquisition
- possession
- trade
- transportation in any form
- medical prescription
- supply
- application
- use
- consumption
- in general, any act related to psychotropic substances

In addition, Article 249 refers to authorizations for scientific purposes regarding the acquisition of such substances, but there are no records of such authorizations for psilocybin.

History of Incidents, Disputes, and Public Reputation

Taking the Doctoriala data, I extract 127 reviews with only 3 flagged as negatives, but are related to cancellations without prior notice and schedule changes, but not with the therapies.

The majority of the reviews on Google Maps and comments on Facebook posts are positive and in gratitude for their services.

But I identified in X an account dedicated to posting about psychology charlatans this account have a post with a direct mention of the target, with name and pictures (used in facebook and Doctoralia), noting that the facility offers services for which it lacks training, as well as procedures and therapies with no real basis.

Also I search for the target's name, and his 3 CURPs in platforms that allow users to search for legal records and case files, but none related to the subject were identified.
